Client Research

This section of research helps to understand the goals and website decisions of Meg's House.

Below are images from Meg's House current website, of things that are mostly not working, but there are some elements in there that are working. Things that are not working for Meg's House is the color selection, which is a maroon purple shade that doesn't give the audience the feeling of comfort that you would want to give someone struggling with domestic violence. Also, when you initally go to the website, you are greeted with having to download Adobe Flash player becuase they have one of their YouTube videos on the homepage of their site which is not a good way to increase traffic on the website. Another issue on Meg's website is the fact that they seem to only use HTML, there is not much CSS involved at all so the site feels very static. Sinces Meg's House is intended to help women and children more forward in their lives, the website should have the ability to make women feel like they are about to overcome something.

Now, after looking at some of the bad design choices of Meg's House, lets look at some of the positives. Meg's House is sure to let their audience know that they are a safe zone, which is a very important aspect of this non-profit. On the website, there is also a Donate Now button which is very beneficial and can be seen from any page you are on. They also keep what seems to be a very updated list of things that they need for the women and children they are helping. Meg's House is also good about including their contact information on multiple pages in case someone needs immediate help. These are definitely things that I intend to keep on the updated website, because this is one of the most important parts of the site.

This section of research is showing statistics and possible solutions.

Step 1: Explore the most recent news in your clients field



One of the leading issues in domestic violence right now is living in a pandemic. It makes it very difficult for victims to get away from their abusers.

Growing evidence shows the pandemic has made intimate partner violence more common—and often more severe. “COVID doesn’t make an abuser,” says Jacky Mulveen, project manager of Women’s Empowerment and Recovery Educators, an advocacy and support group in Birmingham, England. “But COVID exacerbates it. It gives them more tools, more chances to control you. The abuser says, ‘You can’t go out; you’re not going anywhere,’ and the government also is saying, ‘You have to stay in.”

Step 2: Try to find recent innovations in your client's field



What is needed is a preventative approach that allows for the dismantling of intersecting systems of oppression that leave women vulnerable. First, we must provide education on healthy relationships at an early age. Second, leaving an abusive partner, even during a pandemic, needs to be a real option, which means increasing affordable housing, shelter space, access to financial support and culturally relevant healthcare. Finally, prevention strategies should be focused both on potential victims and on potential abusers, reflecting a societal shift away from victim blaming.

According to Crosby, “often what we do is we place the burden on the people who are being abused to get out.” She says that any policy changes and public messaging need instead to let victims know “it’s not your fault that people are processing their anger in this way. It’s not your fault that people aren’t able to face their own trauma.”

Step 4: Any data that is relevant

Research shows that certain shades of blues and greens can relieve anxiety, and using these colors on the website can help women find comfort and peace while they are trying to take the next step in getting out of a difficult and draining situation. Another thing I have found while doing reseach is that 1 in 4 women will deal with domestic violence at some point in their lives and keeping this information on the website will make sure that the audience knows they aren't in this alone. According to research, young women, ages 16 to 24, experience the highest rate of domestic violence, so this means the redesign of Meg's House needs to also be able to reach a younger crowd. These groups of women need to be able to come to this site and feel included and like it was made for them. Gathering this information has helped determine really important aspects of this redesign.


Interviews

This section is devoted to the two interviews that were from the client and a user.

Client Interview

I was able to interview Whitney Marcengil, who is the director of finance at Meg's House and she was able to give me a look into Meg's House, their website, and how their comapny runs as a whole. While talking with Whitney, she mentioned that she basically only works with the financial side and not much of the women and children seeking help. However, she does handle to donation money and mentioned that people more often donated money instead of things off the wishlist on the website. Even though she doesn't work directly with the public, she was able to inform me that people do not usually use their website and it does not seem to be updated frequently. She did mention that it would be beneficial to update the website more often to help women who are mostly seeking shelter, helping hands, and a safe place. Also, Whitney discussed with me that women are referred to Meg's House through case managers and normally stay in their shelter, however that is closed due to Covid. We did discuss that overall Meg's House is in need of more donations and funding and hopefully through the redesign, the website will be more active helping with this issue.

User Interview

For my user interview, I was able to interview Kristen Roberson who talked with me about how she would handle a friend struggling with domestic violence. I spoke with her about what steps she would most likley go through if she encountered this situation. First, she began stating that she woul want to reach out for help. Probaly not knowing where to look, she would go to Google to begin her search in hopes that she would get some answers. Assuming taht she came across the Meg's House webstie, she would be navigating to a hotline phone numner or the contact information of an employee whome she could trust and talk through the situation with. Knowing this information, I believe it is impotant to have the contact infomration or a call to action button that is easy to find for someone in a situation like this.

We also went though the senario of what she would be looking for if she wanted to help the public struggling with domestic violence, including women and children. I asked here where on a webpage she checks first for information and she mentioned the initial main text and the navigation bar so it will be important for this section to have links staright to the donate page, wishlist, and resourse page. After talking thorugh these things she mostly mentioned that it would be really beneficial to be able to find things quickly in the event of an emergency or trying to get help to the public.
